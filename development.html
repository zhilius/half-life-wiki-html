Development History

Half-Life was the debut title of Valve Corporation, founded in 1996 by former Microsoft employees Gabe Newell and Mike Harrington. The game was in development for approximately two and a half years before its release in November 1998.

Origins

Valve licensed the Quake engine from id Software in 1996. The team initially planned to create a standard first-person shooter, but as development progressed, they became focused on creating a more narrative-driven experience that would push the boundaries of the genre.

The game was originally showcased under the working title "Quiver" before the team settled on "Half-Life" — a name that references both the scientific concept of radioactive decay and the protagonist's career trajectory.

Technology

Half-Life runs on the GoldSrc engine, a heavily modified version of the Quake engine. Key technical innovations included:

  • Scripted sequences — Events triggered by player proximity that drive the narrative
  • Skeletal animation — More realistic character movement than contemporary games
  • Seamless world design — No loading screens between connected areas (within chapters)
  • Advanced AI — Enemies that take cover, coordinate, and react to the environment

The GoldSrc engine would later be updated and rebranded as the Source engine for Half-Life 2.

Scripted Sequences

One of Half-Life's most significant innovations was its use of scripted sequences. Unlike traditional cutscenes that take control away from the player, Half-Life's scripted events occur in real-time within the game world. The player remains in full control while events unfold around them, creating a seamless and immersive experience.

Timeline

  1. 1996 — Valve founded; Quake engine license acquired
  2. 1997 — Half-Life announced to the public; original E3 demo shown
  3. 1998 (January) — Original release date missed; development continues
  4. 1998 (November 19) — Half-Life released worldwide
  5. 1999 — Half-Life: Opposing Force released
  6. 2000 — Half-Life: Blue Shift and Half-Life: Decay released

Reception and Awards

Half-Life won over 50 Game of the Year awards and received universal critical acclaim. It is frequently cited as one of the greatest video games of all time, credited with revolutionizing the first-person shooter genre through its emphasis on narrative, immersion, and scripted storytelling.

Quick Facts

  • Development cost approximately $5.5 million.
  • The game shipped on a single CD-ROM.
  • Original working title was "Quiver".
  • Valve had only 7 employees at the start of development.
