What is Half-Life?

Overview

Half-Life is a science fiction first-person shooter developed by Valve Corporation and released in 1998. The game places players in the role of Dr. Gordon Freeman, a theoretical physicist who must fight his way out of the Black Mesa Research Facility after a catastrophic experiment opens a dimensional rift to the alien world of Xen.

Renowned for its immersive storytelling, naturalistic level design, and lack of cutscenes, Half-Life is widely regarded as one of the most influential video games ever made. It won over 50 Game of the Year awards and established Valve as a major force in the gaming industry.

Legacy

Half-Life spawned a franchise including the expansions Opposing Force, Blue Shift, and Decay, the groundbreaking sequel Half-Life 2 (2004), and numerous episodes and spin-offs. The game's GoldSrc engine later evolved into the Source engine, which powered Valve's subsequent titles for years to come.

Technical Innovation

Half-Life was one of the first games to use scripted sequences to drive narrative, seamlessly blending gameplay with storytelling. The game never takes control away from the player — all story events occur in real-time within the game world, a revolutionary approach in 1998.

Did You Know?

  • The name "Half-Life" refers to the scientific concept of radioactive decay, as well as the half-life of Gordon Freeman's career at Black Mesa.
  • The crowbar became one of gaming's most iconic melee weapons and a symbol of the Half-Life series.
  • Valve was founded by former Microsoft employees Gabe Newell and Mike Harrington in 1996.
  • The G-Man appears throughout the game watching the player, but his true nature remains mysterious.
